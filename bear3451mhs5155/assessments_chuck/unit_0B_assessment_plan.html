Precalculus Readiness – Unit B: Exponents, Radicals, and Lines

Essential Standard

Students will apply exponent properties, radical notation, rational exponents, and linear function representations in order to simplify expressions, evaluate quantities, write equations, and prepare for advanced function analysis.
Section B.1: Exponent Properties

Learning Target: Students will simplify expressions using properties of integer exponents.

  • I can apply product, quotient, and power rules for exponents.
  • I can simplify expressions involving zero and negative exponents.
  • I can rewrite expressions using positive exponents.
  • I can explain how exponent properties follow from repeated multiplication.
Section B.2: Radicals and Rational Exponents

Learning Target: Students will convert between radical notation and rational exponent notation and interpret the meaning of fractional exponents.

  • I can rewrite radicals using rational exponents.
  • I can rewrite rational exponents using radical notation.
  • I can identify the root and power in expressions such as x^(m/n).
  • I can explain why x^(1/n) represents an nth root.
Section B.3: Simplifying and Evaluating Radical Expressions

Learning Target: Students will simplify and evaluate radical expressions using structure, perfect powers, and exponent reasoning.

  • I can evaluate square roots, cube roots, and higher roots exactly when possible.
  • I can simplify radical expressions by identifying perfect-square or perfect-cube factors.
  • I can simplify expressions involving rational exponents.
  • I can determine when a radical expression is defined over the real numbers.
Section B.4: Linear Equations from Points, Slopes, Graphs, and Tables

Learning Target: Students will write and interpret linear equations using slope, intercepts, points, graphs, and tables.

  • I can write a linear equation from a point and a slope.
  • I can write a linear equation from two points.
  • I can write a linear equation from a graph or table.
  • I can interpret slope and intercepts in mathematical or real-world contexts.
Section B.5: Algebraic Structure and Function Readiness

Learning Target: Students will connect algebraic fluency to function notation, equations, and multiple representations.

  • I can evaluate expressions and functions using substitution.
  • I can simplify expressions before evaluating or solving.
  • I can identify structure in expressions to choose an efficient strategy.
  • I can connect equations, tables, graphs, and verbal descriptions.

Assessment Methods by DOK Level

DOK 1 — Rungs 1-2

Format: 3–5 minute warm-up, quick check, or exit ticket

Purpose: Check whether students can recall algebraic vocabulary, recognize exponent and radical forms, evaluate simple expressions, and identify basic linear features.

Assessment Ideas

  1. Exponent Property Recognition Check Can Be Assessed After Unit 2, Section 1 – Students identify which exponent property is being used in examples such as x^3x^5, x^7/x^2, (x^2)^4, x^0, and x^-3.
  2. Radical and Rational Exponent Match Can Be Assessed After Unit 2, Section 2 – Students match radical expressions such as sqrt(x), cubert(x^2), and fourthroot(x^3) to rational exponent forms.
  3. Exact Radical Evaluation Check Can Be Assessed After Unit 2, Section 3 – Students evaluate basic radicals and rational exponents such as sqrt(49), cubert(-8), 16^(1/2), and 27^(2/3).
  4. Linear Feature Identification Check Can Be Assessed After Unit 2, Section 4 – Students identify slope, y-intercept, or whether a relationship is linear from a simple equation, table, or graph.
What this tells the teacher:
Students who struggle here need more direct practice with notation, vocabulary, exponent rules, radical meaning, and basic linear features before moving into multi-step simplification or function work.
DOK 2 — Rungs 3-4

Format: Exit ticket, partner check, whiteboard response, or short written explanation

Purpose: Check whether students can apply procedures accurately, simplify expressions, evaluate radicals and rational exponents, and write linear equations from given information.

Assessment Ideas

  1. Exponent Simplification Task Can Be Assessed After Unit 2, Section 1 – Students simplify expressions involving multiple exponent properties, including zero and negative exponents, and rewrite final answers using positive exponents.
  2. Radical Conversion and Simplification Task Can Be Assessed After Unit 2, Section 3 – Students convert between radical and rational exponent notation, then simplify or evaluate expressions using perfect powers.
  3. Domain of Radical Expression Check Can Be Assessed After Unit 2, Section 3 – Students determine whether simple square-root, cube-root, or rational-exponent expressions are defined over the real numbers and explain restrictions.
  4. Linear Equation Writing Task Can Be Assessed After Unit 2, Section 4 – Students write equations from a point and slope, two points, a graph, or a table, then interpret the slope and intercept.
What this tells the teacher:
Students at this level can generally perform algebraic procedures, but the assessment reveals whether they can choose the right rule, maintain notation, and connect algebraic work to graphical or contextual meaning.
DOK 3 — Rungs 5-6

Format: Constructed response, error analysis, gallery walk, or group explanation

Purpose: Check whether students can reason strategically, justify algebraic steps, compare representations, and explain structure rather than relying only on memorized rules.

Assessment Ideas

  1. Exponent Error Analysis Can Be Assessed After Unit 2, Section 1 – Students critique incorrect simplification such as (x^3)^2=x^5, x^-2=-x^2, or x^0=0, identify the misconception, and correct the work.
  2. Radical Structure Explanation Can Be Assessed After Unit 2, Section 3 – Students simplify a radical or rational exponent expression and explain how perfect powers, roots, and exponent rules support each step.
  3. Linear Representation Comparison Can Be Assessed After Unit 2, Section 4 – Students compare a table, graph, and equation for a linear relationship and explain how slope and intercept appear in each representation.
  4. Strategy Selection Task Can Be Assessed After Unit 2, Section 5 – Students are given mixed algebraic expressions and must choose whether exponent rules, radical simplification, rational exponent conversion, substitution, or linear reasoning is most useful. They must justify their choices.
What this tells the teacher:
This level shows whether students understand the structure behind algebraic procedures. These tasks are useful for deciding who is ready for function notation and transformations and who needs reteaching on exponent meaning, radicals, or linear representations.
DOK 4 — Rungs 7-8

Format: Performance task, extended response, modeling task, or strategy defense

Purpose: Check whether students can synthesize algebraic fluency, radical and exponent structure, and linear modeling to solve unfamiliar problems and justify decisions.

Assessment Ideas

  1. Algebraic Modeling Task Can Be Assessed After Unit 2, Section 5 – Students model a situation using a linear equation or expression involving exponents or radicals, interpret the variables, and justify whether the result is reasonable.
  2. Multiple Strategy Simplification Task Can Be Assessed After Unit 2, Section 3 – Students simplify or evaluate a complex expression using more than one method, compare the strategies, and defend which method is clearer or more efficient.
  3. Representation Transfer Task Can Be Assessed After Unit 2, Section 5 – Students move between a verbal description, table, graph, and equation, then explain how each representation shows the same relationship.
  4. Critique and Redesign Task Can Be Assessed After Unit 2, Section 5 – Students evaluate a flawed algebraic solution or linear model, identify the error, revise the work, and explain why the corrected version is valid.
What this tells the teacher:
DOK 4 tasks show whether students can transfer algebraic skills into unfamiliar settings and defend reasoning. These are useful as short performance checks or readiness tasks before Unit 1.

Holistic Rubric

A — Highly Proficient

The student demonstrates a strong and flexible understanding of Unit B concepts. Work is accurate, well-organized, and supported by clear reasoning. The student can simplify and evaluate expressions involving exponent properties, radicals, and rational exponents; write and interpret linear equations from multiple representations; explain restrictions and structure; and justify strategy choices using precise mathematical language.

B — Proficient

The student demonstrates solid understanding of the essential Unit B concepts. Most work is accurate, and the student can complete standard and moderately complex tasks independently. The student can apply exponent rules, convert between radicals and rational exponents, simplify radical expressions, evaluate expressions, and write linear equations from common information. Explanations are generally clear, though they may lack some precision or depth. Errors are minor or isolated and do not show major misconceptions.

C — Developing Proficiency

The student demonstrates partial but passable understanding of Unit B concepts. The student can complete many routine tasks but may need support with multi-step simplification, negative or rational exponents, radical restrictions, equation writing, or connecting representations. Work may contain errors, incomplete explanations, or inconsistent notation, but there is enough evidence that the student understands the main ideas.

Not Yet — Insufficient Evidence

The student does not yet demonstrate sufficient understanding of the essential Unit B concepts. Work shows major errors, missing reasoning, incorrect use of exponent properties, confusion between radicals and rational exponents, difficulty simplifying expressions, or weak understanding of slope and linear equations. The student may need reteaching, additional practice, or reassessment to demonstrate proficiency.
