Precalculus – Unit 04: Trigonometry

Essential Standard

Students will analyze trigonometric relationships and functions in order to model periodic behavior, interpret structure, and justify solutions.
Section 4.1: Unit Circle

Learning Target: Students will use the unit circle to determine exact trigonometric values in radians.

  • I can draw special angles on the unit circle using radian measure.
  • I can determine exact values of sine, cosine, and tangent using the unit circle.
  • I can use unit circle coordinates to find exact values of all six trigonometric functions.
Section 4.2: Sum, Difference, and Pythagorean Identities

Learning Target: Students will apply sum, difference, and Pythagorean identities to simplify and verify trigonometric expressions.

  • I can apply Pythagorean identities to simplify trigonometric expressions.
  • I can apply sum and difference identities to evaluate or rewrite trigonometric expressions.
  • I can verify trigonometric identities algebraically using known identities.
Section 4.3: Half Angle and Double Angle Identities

Learning Target: Students will apply half-angle and double-angle identities to rewrite and evaluate trigonometric expressions.

  • I can apply double-angle identities to simplify or evaluate trigonometric expressions.
  • I can apply half-angle identities to simplify or evaluate trigonometric expressions.
  • I can choose appropriate trigonometric identities to rewrite expressions efficiently.
Section 4.4: Graphing and Writing Trig Functions

Learning Target: Students will graph and write sine and cosine functions using transformations and radian measure.

  • I can graph sine and cosine functions using the five-point method.
  • I can identify amplitude, period, phase shift, and vertical shift of sine and cosine functions.
  • I can write equations of sine and cosine functions from graphs.
Section 4.5: Solving Trig Equations

Learning Target: Students will solve trigonometric equations involving sine, cosine, and tangent.

  • I can solve trigonometric equations involving sine, cosine, and tangent.
  • I can find all solutions to trigonometric equations within a given interval.
  • I can use identities to solve more complex trigonometric equations.

Assessment Methods by DOK Level

DOK 1 — Rungs 1–2

Format: 3–5 minute warm-up, quick check, or exit ticket

Purpose: Check whether students can identify special angles, evaluate trigonometric functions on the unit circle, and use exact values correctly.

Assessment Ideas

  1. Unit Circle Fluency Check Can Be Assessed After Unit 6, Section 1 – Students identify coordinates and exact values of sine, cosine, and tangent for standard angles in radians.
  2. Trig Value Match Task Can Be Assessed After Unit 6, Section 5 – Students match exact trigonometric values to corresponding angles on the unit circle.
  3. Radians and Angle Recognition Check Can Be Assessed After Unit 6, Section 5 – Students convert between degree and radian measure and identify coterminal angles.
  4. Exact Value Evaluation Check Can Be Assessed After Unit 6, Section 5 – Students evaluate expressions involving sine, cosine, and tangent using exact values.
What this tells the teacher:
Students who struggle here need additional fluency practice with the unit circle, radian measure, and exact trigonometric values before moving into identities or graphing.
DOK 2 — Rungs 3-4

Format: Exit ticket, partner check, whiteboard response, or short written explanation

Purpose: Check whether students can graph trigonometric functions, solve equations, and apply identities using procedural understanding and graphical interpretation.

Assessment Ideas

  1. Trig Equation Solving Task Can Be Assessed After Unit 6, Section 5 – Students solve equations involving sine, cosine, or tangent over a specified interval.
  2. Five-Point Graphing Task Can Be Assessed After Unit 6, Section 4 – Students graph sine or cosine functions using amplitude, period, phase shift, and vertical shift.
  3. Transformation Identification Check Can Be Assessed After Unit 6, Section 4 – Students identify transformations from equations or graphs of trigonometric functions.
  4. Identity Simplification Task Can Be Assessed After Unit 6, Section 5 – Students simplify trigonometric expressions using Pythagorean, sum, difference, double-angle, or half-angle identities.
What this tells the teacher:
Students at this level can generally carry out procedures, but the assessment reveals whether they understand how algebraic structure connects to periodic graph behavior and trig relationships.
DOK 3 — Rungs 5-6

Format: Constructed response, error analysis, gallery walk, or group explanation

Purpose: Check whether students can reason strategically, justify identities, explain graph structure, and connect multiple representations of trigonometric relationships.

Assessment Ideas

  1. Identity Verification Task Can Be Assessed After Unit 6, Section 5 – Students verify a trigonometric identity algebraically and explain each transformation step.
  2. Trig Graph Analysis Task Can Be Assessed After Unit 6, Section 4 – Students analyze a transformed sine or cosine graph and explain amplitude, period, phase shift, extrema, and intervals of increase or decrease.
  3. Equation Strategy Comparison Task Can Be Assessed After Unit 6, Section 5 – Students solve a trigonometric equation using two methods and compare the efficiency and reliability of each strategy.
  4. Error Analysis Response Can Be Assessed After Unit 6, Section 5 – Students critique an incorrect trigonometric identity proof or trig equation solution, identify the misconception, and write a corrected explanation.
What this tells the teacher:
This level reveals whether students truly understand the structure of trigonometric relationships rather than simply memorizing procedures. These tasks help determine who is ready for advanced modeling or proof-based reasoning.
DOK 4 — Rungs 7-8

Format: Performance task, extended response, modeling task, or strategy defense

Purpose: Check whether students can synthesize unit-circle reasoning, identities, graphing, and equation solving to model periodic behavior and justify conclusions.

Assessment Ideas

  1. Periodic Modeling Task Can Be Assessed After Unit 6, Section 5 – Students model a real-world periodic situation using a sine or cosine function, interpret key features, and justify the reasonableness of the model.
  2. Design-a-Function Task Can Be Assessed After Unit 6, Section 5 – Students create a trigonometric function satisfying given conditions involving amplitude, period, phase shift, intercepts, and extrema.
  3. Strategy Defense Task Can Be Assessed After Unit 6, Section 5 – Students solve a complex trigonometric equation using multiple approaches and defend which method is most efficient and mathematically appropriate.
  4. Critique and Redesign Task Can Be Assessed After Unit 6, Section 5 – Students evaluate flawed trigonometric reasoning involving identities, graphing, or solutions and revise the work into a valid mathematical argument.
What this tells the teacher:
DOK 4 tasks reveal whether students can transfer trigonometric understanding to unfamiliar situations and defend mathematical reasoning with precision. These are best used as performance assessments, modeling extensions, or summative-level tasks.

Summative Assessment Plan

Unit 4 Summative Assessment Unit 4 Assessed During Unit 6

Assessment is designed for a 45-minute paper-only time limit and is built around balanced evidence rather than a fixed question-count breakdown. Each version includes a mix of procedural fluency, graphing/application tasks, and short reasoning or critique prompts aligned to the unit learning targets and Depth of Knowledge expectations.

Assessments emphasize algebraic accuracy, interpretation of mathematical structure, graphing and representation skills, explanation of reasoning, and application of concepts in both familiar and unfamiliar situations.

Summative assessments are intentionally focused on DoK 1–3 evidence within a paper-only environment. Extended DoK 4 modeling, investigation, performance, or technology-based tasks are assessed separately through activities, investigations, discourse tasks, or formative performance assessments rather than embedded directly into the summative exam.

Holistic Rubric

A — Highly Proficient

The student demonstrates a strong and flexible understanding of Unit 4 concepts. Work is accurate, well-organized, and supported by clear reasoning. The student can use the unit circle fluently, verify identities, graph and analyze trigonometric functions, solve equations using multiple strategies, and justify conclusions using precise mathematical language.

B — Proficient

The student demonstrates solid understanding of the essential Unit 4 concepts. Most work is accurate, and the student can complete standard and moderately complex tasks independently. The student can evaluate trigonometric functions, solve equations, graph transformed trig functions, and apply identities with generally clear reasoning. Errors are minor or isolated and do not show major misconceptions.

C — Developing Proficiency

The student demonstrates partial but passable understanding of Unit 4 concepts. The student can complete many routine tasks but may need support with multi-step equations, identity verification, graph analysis, or connecting representations. Work may contain errors, incomplete explanations, or inconsistent notation, but there is enough evidence that the student understands the main ideas.

Not Yet — Insufficient Evidence

The student does not yet demonstrate sufficient understanding of the essential Unit 4 concepts. Work shows major errors, missing reasoning, incorrect use of identities, confusion with unit-circle values or transformations, or difficulty interpreting trigonometric graphs and equations. The student may need reteaching, additional practice, or reassessment to demonstrate proficiency.
