Precalculus – Unit 01: Function Foundations

Essential Standard

Students will analyze, represent, and transform functions using multiple representations in order to interpret structure, behavior, and solutions algebraically and graphically.
Section 1.1: Function Notation & Solving

Learning Target: Students will evaluate functions and solve equations of the form f(x) = a using tables, algebraic and graphical methods.

  • I can evaluate a function written in function notation for given input values.
  • I can solve equations of the form f(x) = a algebraically when f(x) is linear or a factorable quadratic,.
  • I can solve equations of the form f(x) = a graphically or with a table.
  • I can verify and interpret solutions within the context of the function and its graph.
Section 1.2: Interval & Inequality Notation

Learning Target: Students will represent and interpret sets using interval or inequality notation and graphically on a number line.

  • I can translate between inequalities and interval notation.
  • I can represent solution sets graphically on a number line.
  • I can determine whether endpoints are included or excluded and justify my reasoning.
Section 1.3: Domain & Range

Learning Target: Students will determine and justify the domain and range of functions using graphical and algebraic reasoning.

  • I can determine the domain of a function from its graph or equation.
  • I can determine the range of a function from its graph or equation.
  • I can explain restrictions in domain and range based on function structure.
Section 1.4: Transformations of Parent Functions

Learning Target: Students will analyze and apply transformations of parent functions using the form a*f(x - h) + k.

  • I can identify and describe vertical and horizontal shifts of parent functions.
  • I can determine the effects of reflections and vertical stretches or compressions on parent functions.
  • I can sketch transformed functions using structural reasoning or write an equation based on a graph.
Section 1.5: Limits Graphically

Learning Target: Students will analyze limits graphically by examining how functions behave as x approaches a value from the left and right.

  • I can determine left-hand and right-hand limits from a graph.
  • I can determine whether a limit exists using left-hand and right-hand limits near jumps, holes, and vertical asymptotes.
  • I can distinguish between the limit of a function and the actual function value shown on a graph.

Assessment Methods by DOK Level

DOK 1 — Rungs 1–2

Format: 3–5 minute warm-up, quick check, or exit ticket

Purpose: Check whether students can recall vocabulary, evaluate functions, read basic graph features, and use notation accurately without needing multi-step reasoning.

Assessment Ideas

  1. Function Notation Fluency Check Can Be Assessed After Unit 3, Section 1 – Students evaluate 2–3 functions such as f(3), g(−2), or h(a), then write one sentence explaining what the input and output represent.
  2. Domain and Range Identification Check Can Be Assessed After Unit 3, Section 3 – Students are given a simple graph or equation and identify the domain or range using interval notation.
  3. Transformation Vocabulary Match Can Be Assessed After Unit 3, Section 4 – Students match equations such as f(x)+3, f(x−4), −f(x), and 2f(x) to transformation descriptions.
  4. Limit Reading Check Can Be Assessed After Unit 3, Section 5 – Students read a graph and identify a left-hand limit, right-hand limit, or function value at a point.
What this tells the teacher:
Students who struggle here need more direct practice with notation, vocabulary, and graph reading before moving into explanation-based tasks.
DOK 2 — Rungs 3–4

Format: Exit ticket, partner check, whiteboard response, or short written explanation

Purpose: Check whether students can apply procedures in context, connect representations, and explain a decision using correct mathematical language.

Assessment Ideas

  1. Notation Translation Task Can Be Assessed After Unit 3, Section 2 – Students convert between inequality notation, interval notation, and number-line graphs. They must justify endpoint inclusion or exclusion.
  2. Graphical Limit Decision Can Be Assessed After Unit 3, Section 5 – Students determine left-hand and right-hand limits and whether a limit exists.
  3. Transformation Identification from Equation or Graph Can Be Assessed After Unit 3, Section 4 – Students identify shifts, reflections, and vertical stretches/compressions from a transformed equation or graph.
  4. Solve f(x)=a Two Ways Can Be Assessed After Unit 3, Section 1 – Students solve a simple equation algebraically and confirm it graphically or with a table.
What this tells the teacher:
Students at this level can usually perform the skill, but the formative check reveals whether they understand when and why a representation or method is appropriate.
DOK 3 — Rungs 5–6

Format: Constructed response, gallery walk, error analysis, or group explanation

Purpose: Check whether students can reason strategically, justify conclusions, compare representations, and explain structure.

Assessment Ideas

  1. Domain Restriction Explanation Can Be Assessed After Unit 3, Section 3 – Students determine domain and range, then explain why restrictions occur using function structure, graph behavior, or context.
  2. Transformation Analysis Task Can Be Assessed After Unit 3, Section 4 – Students compare a parent function and a transformed function, then explain how a, h, and k affect structure, key features, and graph behavior.
  3. Limit Reasoning Task Can Be Assessed After Unit 3, Section 5 – Students analyze graphs with holes, jumps, point values, and vertical asymptotes, then explain why limits exist or fail to exist.
  4. Error Analysis Response Can Be Assessed After Unit 3, Section 5 – Students critique an incorrect solution, identify the misconception, and write a corrected explanation.
What this tells the teacher:
This level shows whether students truly understand the mathematics behind the procedures. These tasks are ideal for deciding who is ready for extension or who needs reteaching.
DOK 4 — Rungs 7–8

Format: Performance task, extended response, modeling task, or strategy defense

Purpose: Check whether students can synthesize multiple concepts, make decisions, justify a model, and evaluate reasonableness.

Assessment Ideas

  1. Function Modeling Task Can Be Assessed After Unit 3, Section 5 – Students model a situation using a function, choose an appropriate representation, explain key features, and evaluate whether the model is reasonable.
  2. Design-a-Graph Task Can Be Assessed After Unit 3, Section 5 – Students create a graph that satisfies several conditions involving domain, range, transformations, and limit behavior, then justify their design.
  3. Strategy Defense Task Can Be Assessed After Unit 3, Section 5 – Students solve a multi-step function problem using more than one method and defend which method is more efficient or more reliable.
  4. Critique and Redesign Task Can Be Assessed After Unit 3, Section 5 – Students evaluate a flawed mathematical argument involving domain, transformations, or limits, then revise it into a correct solution.
What this tells the teacher:
DOK 4 tasks show whether students can transfer learning to unfamiliar situations. These are best used sparingly, often as short performance checks or unit-level extensions.

Holistic Rubric

A — Highly Proficient

The student demonstrates a strong and flexible understanding of Unit 1 concepts. Work is accurate, well-organized, and supported by clear reasoning. The student can move between algebraic, graphical, tabular, and verbal representations and can justify conclusions using appropriate mathematical language. Minor errors, if present, do not interfere with the overall reasoning.

B — Proficient

The student demonstrates solid understanding of the essential Unit 1 concepts. Most work is accurate, and the student can complete standard and moderately complex tasks independently. Explanations are generally clear, though they may lack some precision or depth. Errors are minor or isolated and do not show major misconceptions.

C — Developing Proficiency

The student demonstrates partial but passable understanding of Unit 1 concepts. The student can complete many routine tasks but may need support with multi-step reasoning, justification, or connecting representations. Work may contain errors, incomplete explanations, or inconsistent notation, but there is enough evidence that the student understands the main ideas.

Not Yet — Insufficient Evidence

The student does not yet demonstrate sufficient understanding of the essential Unit 1 concepts. Work shows major errors, missing reasoning, incorrect notation, or confusion between key ideas such as function value vs. limit, domain vs. range, or transformation effects. The student may need reteaching, additional practice, or reassessment to show proficiency.
