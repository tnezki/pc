Precalculus - Unit 1

Section 1.4 Investigation

Transformations of Parent Functions

Investigation: What do $a$, $h$, and $k$ do to a parent function?

Time: 15-20 minutes

Format: Desmos slider exploration, point tracking, and whole-class discussion

Learning Target: Analyze and apply transformations of parent functions using the form $a f(x-h)+k$.
Investigation Directions

Setup

  • Grouping: Partners or groups of 3.
  • Materials: Student handout, pencil, and one device per group.
  • Technology: Open desmos.com/calculator.
  • Teacher display: Project Desmos during the launch and closure.

Student Goal

Today you will use sliders and point tracking to discover how a transformed function is built from a parent function.

$$g(x)=a f(x-h)+k$$

By the end, you should be able to describe how $a$, $h$, and $k$ affect the graph and how key points move.

Desmos Start

  1. Enter the parent function: $f(x)=x^2$.
  2. Enter the transformation: $g(x)=a f(x-h)+k$.
  3. Create sliders for $a$, $h$, and $k$.
  4. Keep two sliders fixed while changing only one slider at a time.
  5. After each discovery, test your rule with a second parent function such as $f(x)=|x|$ or $f(x)=\sqrt{x}$.

Norms for the Investigation

  • Change only one parameter at a time until the handout tells you to combine them.
  • Record what happens to the graph before writing a rule.
  • Use transformation language: shift, reflect, vertical stretch, vertical compression, left, right, up, and down.
  • For point tracking, describe how an original point $(x,y)$ moves to a new point.

Discussion Questions
  1. Why does $f(x-3)$ shift right even though the expression contains a minus sign?
  2. How are $f(x)+4$ and $f(x+4)$ different?
  3. What does a negative value of $a$ do to the graph?

Class Notes / Final Rule
Teaching Resource 1: Teacher Script

Purpose

This investigation helps students discover the structure of $g(x)=a f(x-h)+k$. Students should leave understanding that $h$ changes inputs horizontally, $k$ changes outputs vertically, and $a$ scales or reflects output values.

Launch: 2 minutes

Say: “Today we are not memorizing transformation rules first. We are going to move one number at a time, collect evidence, and explain what each number does.”

Check: Every group should have $f(x)=x^2$ and $g(x)=a f(x-h)+k$ in Desmos with sliders created.

Investigation: 10-12 minutes

  1. Part 1: Make sure students see that $a=1$, $h=0$, and $k=0$ leaves the parent function unchanged.
  2. Part 2: Ask, “Are the x-values changing, or are the y-values changing?” Students should connect $k$ to vertical movement.
  3. Part 3: When students say $x+3$ means right 3, ask, “What value of $x$ makes the inside equal to 0?”
  4. Part 4: Press for precise language: $a<0$ reflects over the $x$-axis; $|a|>1$ is a vertical stretch; $0<|a|<1$ is a vertical compression.
  5. Part 5: Use point tracking to connect the algebraic form to graph construction.

Synthesis: 5 minutes

Final summary: In $g(x)=a f(x-h)+k$, $h$ shifts the graph horizontally, $k$ shifts it vertically, and $a$ controls reflection over the $x$-axis and vertical stretch/compression. A point $(x,y)$ on $f$ moves to $(x+h, ay+k)$ on $g$.

Closure prompt: “Describe $g(x)=-3f(x+2)+5$ and explain what happens to the point $(4,-1)$.”

Common Misconceptions to Watch For

  • Students may reverse horizontal shifts because of the sign inside the parentheses.
  • Students may confuse vertical shifts $f(x)+k$ with horizontal shifts $f(x-h)$.
  • Students may say a negative $a$ shifts the graph down instead of reflecting it over the $x$-axis.
  • Students may describe vertical stretches as horizontal narrowing without connecting it to output values.
  • Students may forget that $a$ and $k$ affect y-values while $h$ affects x-values.
Teaching Resource 2: Answer Key
Part 1: Parent Function Baseline

Question: Set $a=1$, $h=0$, and $k=0$. What do you notice?

Answer: $g(x)=f(x)$, so the transformed graph matches the parent function exactly.

Question: List three easy points on $f(x)=x^2$.

Answer: Sample points include $(-1,1)$, $(0,0)$, and $(1,1)$.

Question: Why are these useful?

Answer: They are easy reference points, so students can see how each transformation moves inputs and outputs.

Discussion Questions
  1. Why does $f(x-3)$ shift right? The input must be 3 larger to make the inside match the original input. Equivalently, the key point $(0,0)$ moves to $(3,0)$.
  2. How are $f(x)+4$ and $f(x+4)$ different? $f(x)+4$ shifts up 4. $f(x+4)$ shifts left 4.
  3. What does negative $a$ do? It reflects the graph over the $x$-axis.
  4. How does point tracking help? It moves known parent-function points to transformed locations without creating a full table.
  5. Student error: $g(x)=-2f(x+5)-1$ shifts left 5, not right 5. It also reflects over the $x$-axis, vertically stretches by 2, and shifts down 1.
