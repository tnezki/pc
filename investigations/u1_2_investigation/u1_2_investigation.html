Precalculus - Unit 1

Section 1.2 Investigation

Interval & Inequality Notation

Investigation: How do inequalities, intervals, and number-line graphs show the same set?

Time: 15-20 minutes

Format: Card sort, endpoint investigation, partner talk, and whole-class discussion

Learning Target: Represent and interpret sets using interval notation, inequality notation, and number-line graphs.
Investigation Directions

Setup

  • Grouping: Partners or groups of 3.
  • Materials: Student handout, pencil
  • Teacher display: Project one number-line graph and one matching interval during the launch.
  • Timing: 5 minutes for card matching, 7-9 minutes for endpoint rule discovery, 5 minutes for discussion and synthesis.

Student Goal

Today you will compare three ways to describe the same set of numbers.

$\text{Inequality} \qquad \text{Interval} \qquad \text{Number-line Graph}$

By the end, you should be able to explain when to use parentheses, brackets, open circles, and closed circles.

Norms for the Investigation

  • Match by meaning, not by symbols only.
  • Ask: “Are the endpoint numbers included?” before choosing a bracket or circle.
  • Use test values to check whether a graph, inequality, and interval describe the same set.
  • Be ready to explain why infinity never gets a bracket.

Discussion Questions
  1. How does the word “at least” help you decide between an open circle and a closed circle?
  2. Why does $(-3,4]$ use a parenthesis on one side and a bracket on the other?
  3. Why do $(-\infty,5)$ and $x<5$ represent the same set?
  4. Why does infinity always use a parenthesis instead of a bracket?
  5. A student says $x\le7$ should be written as $( -\infty,7 )$. What is correct about the student’s thinking, and what needs to be fixed?
Class Notes / Final Rule
Teaching Resource 1: Teacher Script

Purpose

This investigation helps students discover that inequalities, interval notation, and number-line graphs are three representations of the same set of numbers. The main conceptual goal is endpoint inclusion: students should connect inclusive language to closed circles and brackets, and exclusive language to open circles and parentheses.

Launch: 2 minutes

Say: “Today you are not memorizing a notation chart first. You are going to match examples and use those matches to build the rules for yourself.”

Quick display: Show $x\ge2$ and ask students whether $2$ itself is allowed. Then connect that one decision to a closed circle and a bracket.

Investigation: 10-12 minutes

  1. Part 1: Circulate and ask, “Is the endpoint included?” before students fill the interval notation.
  2. Part 2: Push students to use the language of the situation. Words like “at least,” “no more than,” and “including” should trigger inclusion.
  3. Part 3: Do not give the rules too early. Let students use the completed rows to write the rules.
  4. Part 4: Ask students to verify with test values. This prevents symbol-only matching.

Synthesis: 5 minutes

Final summary: A closed circle and a bracket mean the endpoint is included. An open circle and a parenthesis mean the endpoint is not included. Infinity always uses a parenthesis because infinity is a direction, not a number that can be included.

Closure prompt: “Write one set three ways: as an inequality, as interval notation, and as a number-line graph.”

Common Misconceptions to Watch For

  • Students may reverse the direction of a one-sided inequality when converting to interval notation.
  • Students may use brackets with infinity.
  • Students may confuse $<$ with $\le$ when reading verbal phrases.
  • Students may treat $(-3,4]$ as two separate numbers instead of all values between them.
  • Students may write compound inequalities in the wrong order, such as $4<x\le-3$.

Discussion Questions
  1. At least: “At least” means the value can equal the endpoint, so it uses a closed circle or bracket.
  2. $(-3,4]$: $-3$ is not included, but $4$ is included.
  3. $(-\infty,5)$ and $x<5$: Both describe every number less than $5$.
  4. Infinity: Infinity is not a fixed endpoint value, so it cannot be included.
  5. Student error: The student knows the set goes left forever and stops at $7$, but $7$ must be included, so the correct interval is $(-\infty,7]$.
